Agentic AI Doesn't Fix Broken Processes. It Accelerates Them.

The enterprise has declared the pilot phase over. What CTOs are being asked to build on — undocumented processes, ungoverned data, teams already building independently — is the same foundation that broke citizen development. The difference is that agents don't wait for someone to notice something is wrong.

In February 2026, Harvard Business Review published a blueprint for enterprise-wide agentic AI transformation. Among the frameworks and recommendations was a line that had nothing to do with AI capability: "When introduced into a weak or fragmented system, AI doesn't fix the system; it amplifies its flaws." That observation landed the same month Fast Company reported that CIOs have moved past the experimentation and pilot phases and are now deploying agentic AI into core workflows.

Read those two sentences together and the problem becomes clear.

The Pattern Has Run Before

About 18 months ago, the same conversation was happening at a smaller scale. Motivated business users were building their own tools, bypassing IT, solving real problems that had been sitting in backlogs for years. The hunger was legitimate. The tools worked. The problems started when someone wanted to share what they had built.

A personal automation that worked fine on a laptop became a team dependency. The team dependency got pushed to the department. Each expansion exposed what the original build never accounted for: no error handling, no authentication, no logging, no support model, no understanding of what happens when the source data changes at 11pm on a Tuesday.

IT got called. IT hadn't built it. IT couldn't explain it. The bottleneck that citizen development was supposed to eliminate reappeared, wearing different clothes.

That pattern is now repeating at enterprise scale, with agents instead of apps, and the stakes are considerably higher.

Agents Raise the Stakes

A citizen-built app that breaks does so passively. It stops working. Someone notices. A ticket gets filed.

An agentic AI system that goes wrong does something different. It acts. It makes decisions, sends outputs, triggers downstream processes, modifies records, escalates cases, and routes work at a velocity no human oversight can match in real time. Fast Company's reporting on enterprise agentic adoption put it plainly: without the correct guidelines, agentic AI can "race in the wrong direction at a velocity no human could match."

The asymmetry matters. When a vibe-coded app breaks, the damage is contained to the moment it stops. When an agent runs in the wrong direction, the damage accumulates until someone catches it, and catching it requires understanding a system that, like the citizen dev apps before it, was often built without documentation, clear ownership, or a recovery plan.

The Foundation Nobody Built

Fast Company identifies SOPs as the critical prerequisite most organizations are skipping. The argument is not complicated: agents need to be told exactly how to behave in every situation they will encounter, because they cannot navigate ambiguity the way a human employee can. A human who hits an edge case can ask someone. An agent that hits an edge case will resolve it with whatever logic it was given, whether that logic applies or not.

Formalized SOPs became unfashionable over the past decade because people could navigate ambiguity. That flexibility was a feature, not a gap. Agentic AI has no such flexibility. The process documentation organizations never wrote because their people didn't need it has now become load-bearing infrastructure.

The same dynamic applies to data. Agentic systems that do anything meaningful need access to real operational data: customer records, financial transactions, inventory, HR information. They need that data to be accurate, consistently defined, and governed well enough to be safely exposed to an autonomous system. A semantic layer that defines what "active customer" and "pending order" and "approved vendor" actually mean, consistently, across every system the agent might touch.

Most enterprises are still building that layer. The Microsoft Cloud Adoption Framework's enterprise AI checklist lists "Develop an AI data strategy" as a precondition for AI readiness, not an afterthought. The organizations skipping it are not skipping a nice-to-have. They are deploying agents into data environments that will produce confident wrong answers, and those answers will propagate through automated workflows before anyone notices.

Agent Sprawl Is What Comes Next

HBR's agentic AI research names "agent sprawl" as one of three primary failure modes: uncontrolled proliferation of siloed AI agents, built by different teams, solving overlapping problems, with no unified architecture or ownership model.

This is the citizen dev problem, restated. Individual teams, frustrated with slow central processes, build their own agents for their own workflows. Each one works, in isolation, for the team that built it. Multiply that across a large organization and you have dozens of disconnected agents, each touching different slices of the same underlying systems, with no one responsible for how they interact, what they share, or what happens when they conflict.

When one of those agents breaks, or produces an output that creates a compliance problem, a customer-facing error, or a data inconsistency, IT gets called. IT didn't build it. IT doesn't own the vendor relationship, the prompt design, the data connections, or the business logic. They have been handed a problem that requires understanding a system they have never seen, built by someone who may have moved on, governed by documentation that probably does not exist.

The support paradox from the citizen dev era has not been solved. It has been promoted.

The Data Problem Is Still the Data Problem

There is a version of the agentic AI story that goes: the tools are ready, the models are capable, the enterprise just needs to deploy. That version is missing the constraint that makes everything else harder.

The data requirement for agentic AI is not smaller than it was for citizen development. It is larger. An agent that summarizes documents needs clean, accessible documents. An agent that manages customer escalations needs accurate, real-time customer records with consistent definitions. An agent that approves purchase orders needs a vendor data model that the finance system, the procurement system, and the ERP all agree on.

Thirty-seven percent of executives cite data privacy and security as their top concern with agentic AI adoption, per HBR's research. That is the visible risk, the one that makes it onto slide decks. The structural risk is less visible: organizations deploying agents into data environments that were not designed to be queried by autonomous systems, without the lineage, governance, or semantic consistency that makes the outputs trustworthy.

The tools are not the bottleneck. They have not been for some time.

What the CTO Actually Has to Do

The organizations getting this right are not moving faster. They are treating agentic AI deployment as a process and data initiative that happens to use AI, rather than an AI initiative that happens to touch processes and data.

That means three things, each unglamorous and each load-bearing.

Document the processes before the agents run them. Not because documentation is good practice in the abstract, but because an agent deployed into an undocumented process will invent its own interpretation of every edge case. That interpretation will be wrong in ways that are hard to detect and harder to reverse.

Govern the data before connecting agents to it. Governed API layers, consistent semantic definitions, audit logging for every query. The appetite for shortcuts here is understandable: the data work is slow and invisible to the business. The cost of skipping it is an agent producing authoritative-looking outputs from inconsistent inputs.

Establish a unified architecture before teams start building independently. The individual team that builds its own agent for its own workflow is not wrong to want that capability. They are wrong to build without coordinating with the center. Agent sprawl is not a technology failure. It is a governance failure that technology enables.

None of this is new. It is the same work organizations should have done before citizen development, and many didn't. The difference now is that the systems acting on inadequate foundations are not passive. They act. They move fast. They do not wait for someone to notice something is wrong.

That is the argument for getting the foundation right before the agents are running.
